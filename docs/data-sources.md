## 1. Saltwater intrusion (the physical driver)

| Fact | Figure | Source |
|------|--------|--------|
| Rice tolerance ceiling | Most crops tolerate < **1 g/L** salinity; the **4 g/L** contour is the standard "rice-killing" threshold used by Vietnamese agencies | [S1], [S8] |
| 2024-25 dry season | 4 g/L salinity reached **15-20 km** up most river mouths | [S1] |
| 2020 crisis | 4 g/L pushed **~60 km up the Tien River, 40-50 km up the Hau**, ~8-10 km deeper than 2016; affected **10 of 13** delta provinces | [S3], [S9] |
| 2016 crisis (El Niño) | Saltwater pushed **>90 km inland**, destroyed **~240,000 ha** of rice, **~$450M** losses | [S3], [S9] |
| Root cause ranking | **Reduced dry-season upstream discharge** (upstream dams + diversion) is the *principal* driver, stronger than ENSO; compounded by sea-level rise and land subsidence | [S10] |
| Subsidence | Delta sinking **~2-4 cm/yr** from groundwater extraction; most land sits **~1 m** above sea level | [S6] |

**Scenario use:** the "test water at different locations" mechanic maps directly onto the
4 g/L contour moving inland. Near the coast = 10-15 g/L (rice impossible); mid-delta =
2-4 g/L (marginal); upstream = <1 g/L (safe). This is real geography, not invented.

**Seasonality (critical for the decision):** salinity intrusion is a DRY-SEASON phenomenon.
In the wet season, upstream flow and rainfall push the salt back and water turns fresh again.
This seasonal swing is exactly why integrated rice-shrimp works - shrimp in the brackish dry
season, rice in the fresh wet season, on the same field. A solution judged on a single
salinity reading, ignoring season/duration, is agriculturally incomplete. [S3], [S9], [S5]

## 2. Rice yield loss (the harm the student sees)

| Fact | Figure | Source |
|------|--------|--------|
| Tolerance threshold | Rice salt-tolerance threshold **EC = 3-4 dS/m (~1.9-2.56 g/L)** | [S2] |
| Yield penalty | **10-15%** loss above EC 4 dS/m; **20-50%** loss above EC 6 dS/m (~3 g/L) | [S2] |
| Observed field losses | **30-70%** yield loss in Ca Mau, Ben Tre, Tra Vinh, Soc Trang, Kien Giang, Vinh Long during intrusion years | [S2] |
| Absolute loss | **2.5-4.05 tonnes/ha/yr** attributable to saltwater intrusion | [S2] |

**Scenario use:** the farmer's "declining harvest" should be quantified - e.g. "my paddy
gave 6 t/ha, now barely 3." That's within the cited 30-70% range.

## 3. Adaptation options (the tradeoffs the student weighs)

### Salt-tolerant rice
- Promoted via the CURE consortium; widely adopted in salinity-prone zones. Effective
  **up to ~4 g/L** - buys tolerance, does **not** make rice grow in seawater. [S4], [S8]
- Honest tradeoff: keeps farmers in rice/income continuity, but is a *ceiling-raiser*, not
  a cure. Above 4 g/L it fails too.

### Integrated rice-shrimp farming
- Shrimp (brackish, dry season) + rice (freshwater, wet season) on the same field. [S5]
- Income: **~$9,800 (min) to ~$20,000 (avg), up to ~$40,000/ha** in good years;
  yields ~5-8 t rice + 300-1,000 kg shrimp per cycle. [S5]
- Scale: grew to **~153,000 ha by 2014** (~28% of national brackish-shrimp area). [S5]
- Honest tradeoff: higher income *but* higher capital/risk, needs brackish water (so it
  only "wins" where salinity is already too high for rice), and large-scale conversion
  raises sustainability/regulation concerns. This is the farmer-vs-official-vs-researcher tension.

### Crop change (non-rice)
- Dry-season intrusion is actively driving identification of alternative crops to rice. [S7]

## 4. Human / drought dimension (stakes for the impact case)

- 2020 El Niño drought left **~73,900 households** in the delta with limited fresh water. [S11]
- Two severe droughts in a decade (2016, 2020), repeats in 2022 and 2024 - framed by
  researchers as "the new normal." [S12], [S6]
